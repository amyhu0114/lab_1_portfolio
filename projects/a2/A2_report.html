Subtheme: Speculation

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. To what extent is speculation occuring? What type of people/entity are more likely to speculate? (Source: Personal Experience - The landlord of my apartment is an real estate firm that specializes in property acquisition and invesment. Therefore, I'm curious about the extent to which speculation is occuring in the bigger Boston area.)
  2. How does speculation change over time? (Source: Homes for Profit: Speculation and Investment in Greater Boston Report - The report covers data spanning from 2000-2023. Putting housing speculation into context of various historical events such as the burst of the housing bubble in 2008 that occured within the time frame of the study, I was curious to see if and how the extent of speculation changes over time.)
  3. Where do housing speculation occur and what type of houses are most likely to be speculated? (Source: 6.C35J Lecture - In one of the first few lectures, the professor mentioned how speculation especially impacts communities with people of color so I was wondering if certain neighborhoods and housing types are more susceptible to speculation.)
  4. How does the purchase of properties for investor vs. non-investor differ (e.g. cash/mortgage, sale price)? (Source: Homes for Profit: Speculation and Investment in Greater Boston Report - The report mentions that investors and non-investors have different goals in mind when purchasing a property. Therefore, I was curious about the differences between the transactions involving investor vs non-investor buyers.)

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

From the exploratory data analysis of the residential sales data in the city of Boston from 2000-2023, I aimed to gain a better understanding of to what extent, where, and how does speculation occur and change in Boston over time. I learned that investor buyers participate in fewer housing transactions than non-investors, but they tend to invest in higher-priced properties than non-investors and have higher number of total properties attached to their names. Over time, there is a general upward trend of the sum of purchase price for all categories of buyers that fluctuates with societal events, with investors being more sensitive to changes in the economic environment. In terms of geographic locations, while housing transactions involving investor and non-investor buyers both occur in almost all neighborhoods, there are more speculation occurring in neighborhoods such as Back Bay and Downtown Boston than that of neighborhoods such as South End. Moreover, the proportion for cash-only purchase with no mortgage is higher for investor buyers than non-investor buyers - likely due to the availability of more cash on hand for speculators.
